meVBT
Velocity-based training & muscular-fatigue tracking, fused from the sensors you already own.
Working prototype · May 2026

Heart-rate wearables are blind to lifting — you can grind your muscles and CNS to failure with barely a flicker in HR, so they tell you that you hardly trained. meVBT counts every rep and tracks intra-set velocity loss — a validated proximity-to-failure signal — to build the muscular strain & recovery score those platforms structurally can’t.

4 commercial tools → 1 model 3-way agreement to ~0.02 m/s (best case) confidence-scored, per rep watch-first, source-agnostic

The bet

  • Your wrist already wears precise sensors. An Apple Watch IMU (plus AirPods) can recover bar velocity, range of motion, and fatigue — no device strapped to the bar.
  • Multimodal from day one: phone video, BLE bar tools (Vitruve, Stance), and the watch all emit the same shape into one model. Fuse when more than one is present; degrade gracefully when not.
  • Device-agnostic by design: the watch is just the most convenient source — the same model ingests a Fitbit, Garmin, Whoop, or a tape-on IMU. The moat is the fusion model and the muscular-strain layer, not the hardware — so we ride whatever you already wear instead of selling you a band.
  • The wedge isn’t a sensor trick — it’s the muscular strain score HR platforms can’t compute.

Operating philosophy

  • One abstraction: every source → (rep boundaries · velocity · ROM) + confidence.
  • Confidence is per-rep and per-metric — and it’s unclaimed territory. No competitor tells you how much to trust a number.
  • A learned personal rep-shape prior is an always-on “source” that compounds every set — so even messy, watch-only data stays legible.
  • Sources repair each other — agreement on a turnaround sharpens everyone’s velocity. Relative trend beats a perfect absolute, so it works on any lift.

What’s built

  • Native iOS + watchOS capture — a workout session keeps high-rate motion alive and streams it to the phone.
  • A shared Swift core (the source abstraction + confidence model) and a Python ZUPT pipeline — rep detection, velocity, ROM — under test.
  • A living measurement lab: the same sets recorded by SmartBarbell, Stance, Metric & WL Analysis, linked rep-by-rep — with ground-truth Vitruve incoming. 5 sets across squat, bench & deadlift, growing each session.

Early learnings

  • Every “ground truth” disagrees — on one deadlift, pro tools differed on rep count and on how fast the set decayed.
  • The reps that matter most are measured worst — the grindy final reps that carry the fatigue signal are where every source wobbles.
  • In good conditions, signals converge — three independent methods agreed within ~0.02 m/s on incline bench.
  • The error is a near-constant offset, not a moving target — a tractable calibration.
  • Confidence is open white space — a category leader’s “100%” badge stayed 100% while it silently missed half a set.

Roadmap

  1. Calibrate the watch against Vitruve ground truth (deadlift first); quantify the error.
  2. Ship confidence + personal prior + velocity-loss — make watch-only trustworthy.
  3. A manual rep editor where every correction trains the prior — the data flywheel.
  4. Fuse a second source (video / AirPods) for consensus rep detection.
  5. The product: a muscular strain & recovery score, for any lift.

Hurdles to clear

  • The wrist isn’t the bar — accuracy is exercise-dependent and drift is the enemy (squat hardest; deadlift & bench best).
  • Sustained sensing: watchOS must hold ~100–200 Hz for a full session without throttling, sleep, or draining the battery — the first thing to prove.
  • Rep detection on grindy and unconventional lifts; clean multi-source time-sync & fusion.
  • The prior needs data — cold-start, then per-user learning at scale.
  • Recording reality — frame rate & setup quietly degrade video; the system must know when to trust what.
